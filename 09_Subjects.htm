Subjects

 

Learning intention

By the end of this page, I can identify the subject of a sentence and explain how subject choice shapes the focus and meaning of a sentence.

 

 

What is it?

The subject of a sentence is the noun, pronoun, or noun phrase that the sentence is about — the thing that is doing, being, or experiencing whatever the verb describes.

Every complete sentence has a subject. Finding it is straightforward: locate the verb first, then ask who or what is doing it.

The students completed the survey.  (Who completed? The students — subject.)

A sense of unease pervades the novel.  (What pervades? A sense of unease — subject.)

She argued the case convincingly.  (Who argued? She — subject.)

The subject can be a single word, a noun phrase, or even a clause:

The government's decision to cut funding caused widespread concern.

(The whole phrase is the subject.)

 

What the author is suggesting is far more complex than it first appears.

(The clause 'what the author is suggesting' is the subject.)

 

Subject choice shapes meaning. Choosing what to make the subject determines whose story this is and how the reader interprets the event.

The police arrested the protesters.

The protesters were arrested.

Thirty activists were detained without charge.

A peaceful demonstration ended in mass arrests.

 

Same event. Four completely different framings.

 

What does it look like?

Read this analytical sentence and notice where the subject is doing important work:

The repeated motif of locked doors suggests that the characters are imprisoned not by their

circumstances but by their own choices.

 

The subject is not a character's name — it's a literary technique.

Making a technique the subject signals that this is analysis, not plot summary.

❌  The characters can't escape because they make bad choices.  (subject = character — plot summary)

✅  The repeated motif of locked doors suggests that imprisonment is psychological rather than physical.

    (subject = literary device — analysis)

 

Try it

Identification task: Underline the subject in each sentence below.

1. The final chapter reframes everything that came before it.

2. A significant number of students reported feeling anxious about the transition.

3. Throughout the novel, the tension between freedom and control drives the narrative.

4. What the data reveals is more complicated than the government's initial response suggested.

5. She had never considered the possibility of failure.

 

Writing task: Write the same observation about a text three times, changing only the subject.

Version 1: a character's name  ·  Version 2: a literary technique  ·  Version 3: an abstract noun

Notice which version sounds most analytical.

 

Take it further

At Year 11 and 12, the most powerful subjects in analytical writing are abstract nouns and nominalised phrases — subjects that name ideas, techniques, or interpretations rather than characters or events.

The tension between individual agency and social constraint is the central concern of the text.

The author's use of fragmented syntax mirrors the protagonist's psychological disintegration.

This juxtaposition invites the reader to question which version of events is reliable.

 

These subjects signal immediately that the writer is operating at the level of ideas, not plot.

In persuasive writing, making an abstract value the subject gives that value prominence and weight.

Fairness demands that we reconsider this policy.

The safety of our communities must come before political convenience.

 

 

Watch out for

Burying the real subject

❌  There is a significant amount of evidence that suggests the policy has failed.

✅  Significant evidence suggests the policy has failed.

There is and it is constructions delay or hide the real subject. Find it and put it first.

 

Losing track of the subject in a long sentence

❌  The impact of these policies on young people in rural and remote communities are significant.

✅  The impact of these policies on young people in rural and remote communities is significant.

The subject is impact (singular), not communities. Trace back to the actual subject before choosing the verb form.

 

Defaulting to character as subject in analytical writing

❌  Hamlet is indecisive and this makes the play interesting.

✅  Hamlet's paralysis functions as a critique of the Renaissance ideal of decisive masculine action.

 

Ask yourself

•        Before you write an analytical sentence, have you asked: what should the subject be — a character, a technique, or an idea?

•        Are there sentences starting with 'there is' or 'it is' where a more direct subject would be stronger?

•        In a long sentence, can you always identify the subject clearly and check that the verb agrees with it?
